Dr. Madhumita Kurdukar #MDKCorner

What is this specimen: This is a formalin fixed partial specimen of the heart with the entire circumference of the arch of aorta.

What can we see: The heart has been cut longitudinally through the apex, left ventricle, and the aorta in order to expose the ventricular cavity, aortic valves and intima of the aorta. A slide has been placed transversely in the ventricular cavity to keep it patent and display the papillary muscles and the chordae tendinae. One cusp of the mitral valve too can be visualised.

What is pathology: The left ventricular wall is hypertrophic (>1.5 cm) and the ventricle appears dilated even after removal of the glass slide. All the three aortic semilunar valves can be seen and are not thickened or calcified. The aorta is cut open longitudinally and the two halves are spread apart thereby exposing its intima and layers. While the entire intima is covered with atheromatous plaques, a yellowish-white, elevated plaque can be prominently appreciated on the right side (as marked). Both halves distinctly show a hematoma in the tunica media separating the layers of the aortic wall. Although the dissection involves the ascending aorta, we cannot identify an intimal tear or aortic root dilation.

What do we infer: This is a specimen of the partial heart and aortic arch showing atherosclerosis and dissecting aneurysm of the aorta with possible dilated cardiomyopathy.

What is your spot diagnosis: Dissecting Aortic Aneurysm and Left Ventricular Hypertrophy

What is this specimen: This is a formalin fixed specimen of the partial left lung.

What can we see: The left lung is identified from cardiac impression and hilar structures in the left image and oblique fissure in the right image. This is not the entire lung, hence the surfaces aren’t clearly discernable, however, the left image broadly shows the mediastinal surface (apex, hilum and the cardiac impression) in the upper part and diaphragmatic surface in the lower part. The image on the right is a longitudinal ‘section’ and should not be mistaken for the costal surface.

What is pathology: In the left image, pleura is visible both on mediastinal and diaphragmatic surfaces. It is markedly thickened, lusterless, opaque, and firmly adherent to the underlying lung (as examined using forceps). Besides this, there are multiple enlarged lymph nodes at the hilum. They are blackish in colour due to anthracotic pigment. In the right image, there is a distinct triangular cavity with irregular margins, having variable depths, located at the apex of the lung, and visible on the cut surface. On careful examination of the cavity, it is empty and the floor is rough, both denoting possibly washed out caseous material while the whitish tissue surrounding the cavity denotes fibrosis. Ergo, the description of fibrocaseous tuberculosis. Overall, the lung parenchyma on the cut surface is blackish (anthracosis) and shows bronchioles cut in different planes.

What do we infer: This is a specimen of partial left lung showing fibrocavitary lesion possibly due to tuberculosis

What is your spot diagnosis: Fibrocavitary Tuberculosis

What is this specimen: These are formalin fixed slices of the lung obtained.

What can we see: These are two non-contiguous slices of possibly the same (ipsilateral) lung. The only characteristic feature of the lung is the presence of bronchi cut in different planes. The hilum, characteristic spongy consistency, and fissures cannot be identified.

What is pathology: The cut surface of the lung shows multiple, tiny, grey-white, millet-sized, nodules that are characteristic of miliary tuberculosis. Few smaller nodules also appear to have been coalesced. The intervening parenchyma is unremarkable. The pleura is thickened as seen on the right slice.

What do we infer: These are non-contiguous slices of the lung showing the characteristic miliary appearance possibly due to tuberculosis.

What is your spot diagnosis: Miliary Tuberculosis

What is this specimen: This is a formalin fixed specimen of the entire kidney.

What can we see: In this specimen the side of the kidney cannot be ascertained due to absence of the hilar structures viz., vein, artery, pelvis (from anterior to posterior). Additionally, the kidney has been vertically bisected in unequal halves due to which the pelvicalyceal system cannot be visualized. Corticomedullary differentiation is preserved and indistinctly visible in certain areas. On both sections, the external surface can be visualized and is covered by a thin, transparent, glistened capsule that is loosely overhanging in some areas.

What is pathology: On both unequal halves, we can see a sectioned growth possibly at the upper pole. We cannot definitively comment on the pole, again due to absence of renal pelvis. On examination of the left section, the growth appears to be circumscribed and surrounded by a pseudo-capsule. composed of small, delicate/friable, papillary excrescences (visualized better when viewed laterally in a formalin-filled jar). Necrosis or haemorrhage is not seen. A satellite nodule of similar morphology is seen just below the main growth only on the left section. A few yellowish orange formalin fixation (of renal sinus fat) artefacts noted on both sections. The rest of the renal parenchy appears unremarkable.

What do we infer: This is a specimen of the entire kidney showing a papillary growth at the upper pole possibly a renal cell carcinoma.

What is your spot diagnosis: Papillary Renal Cell Carcinoma
